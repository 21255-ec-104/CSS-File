HTML's roots can be traced back to the early 1980s when physicist Tim Berners-Lee, working at CERN (the European Organization for Nuclear Research), developed a system to share research information among scientists. He proposed the concept of hypertext, which allowed linking and browsing between documents.

In 1991, Berners-Lee published the first version of HTML in collaboration with his colleague Robert Cailliau. It was known as "HTML Tags" and provided a simple markup language for linking and organizing documents on the internet. Over the years, HTML went through various iterations and updates, with the involvement of the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) - an organization that develops and maintains web standards. HTML4 was introduced in 1997, and HTML5 became the latest and most significant version, officially standardized in 2014. HTML5 brought substantial enhancements, including support for multimedia elements, new structural elements, and improved semantic tags. Lie, a Norwegian web pioneer, proposed the idea of a style sheet language that could control the layout and design of web pages independently of their content. Together with Bert Bos, Lie created the first version of CSS, called "CSS1," in 1996. This language allowed web developers to apply styles to HTML documents and control attributes like fonts, colors, and spacing. CSS1 was followed by CSS2 in 1998, which added more features and capabilities for page layout and design.
